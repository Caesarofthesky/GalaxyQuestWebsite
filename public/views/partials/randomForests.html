Chapter 2: Researching our Path

Forging a path through space requires knowedge of the environment

  • Find out what other people have done already

    • In order to create something new, a great first step is to sniff out what already exists. With the internet being what it is today, it's easier than ever to have access to expert level material. Look for academic research, scholarly articles, youtube lecture videos from universities and presentations. At this stage, you want to be learning a lot of "high-level" material to get a feel for the available resources that you could potentially use. Strive to be able to conduct a presentation on your findings, as this will mean you truely have found and learned the appropriate material.

  • Narrow your focus

    • Once you know what information exists and the body of research supporting it consists of, it is time to narrow down your search. Start digging deeper into some ideas you may have glossed over in your first research sprint. Given your problem and what your expected solution is supposed to provide, now is the time to start deciding on the method you are going to use to rock your problem.

  • Combine your machine and your data

    • When you know what specific algorithm will suit your needs best, you should look at how to implement it with your data. Find out what tools exist to help you with this approach. Some tools come with built in features that may help you in your search for the answers you seek. Take a look at what inputs these tools take and see which ones will work with the existing data set you have already.

Our Learnings and Discovered Best Practices




  • Find out what other people have done already

    • While it took some time to set up our development environment, Sci-kit learn gave us a direction to start working in. It is specifically built for our type of research and included a number of modules for us to use in our Galaxy Quest adventure. There are also a lot of great videos and resources we found to aid educating ourselves about machine learning. Professor Nando de Freitas from the University of British Columbia has an excellent series of lecture videos to start learning with. Thanks Professor de Freitas!

  • Narrow your focus

    • After much research, we decided that we would use a Random Forest regression to solve our problem. For more in-depth information and a kick ass visual on how Random Forests work, check out our visualization page.

  • Combine your machine and your data

    • The tools we chose came with ways of developing features that we needed for our algorithm. In particular, sci-kit image gave us a way to change the size of the images in our dataset and look for certain features within those images, then send them back to our algorithm to do work on. Knowing your dataset and what features you may want to use, as well as what your particular algorithm is good at will help you in this step.
